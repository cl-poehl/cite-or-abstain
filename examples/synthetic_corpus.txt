§3.1.2 — Risk stratification (synthetic)

Localized prostate cancer is stratified into low-, intermediate-, and high-risk
groups. Intermediate-risk disease is further divided into favorable and
unfavorable categories on the basis of ISUP grade, PSA, and the percentage of
positive biopsy cores. Risk group directly informs the choice between active
surveillance and definitive therapy.

§4.2.1 — Active surveillance criteria (synthetic)

Active surveillance is the preferred management approach for ISUP grade group 1
(Gleason 6) prostate cancer in patients with life expectancy greater than ten
years. For ISUP grade group 2 (favorable intermediate-risk) disease, active
surveillance may be considered in selected patients with favorable features
including low PSA density and limited tumor volume; however, definitive therapy
is more commonly recommended at this risk level.

§5.1.3 — PSMA-PET in initial staging (synthetic)

PSMA-PET imaging is recommended for initial staging of unfavorable
intermediate-risk and high-risk localized prostate cancer prior to definitive
therapy. Its role in low-risk and favorable intermediate-risk disease is more
limited and should be considered case-by-case.

§6.4.2 — Systemic therapy at metastatic diagnosis (synthetic)

In patients with newly diagnosed metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate cancer
(mHSPC), intensified systemic therapy at the time of metastatic diagnosis
improves overall survival compared with androgen deprivation therapy (ADT)
monotherapy. Options for intensification include the addition of docetaxel
chemotherapy or of a novel hormonal agent (abiraterone, apalutamide,
enzalutamide, or darolutamide combined with docetaxel). Selection should account
for patient fitness, comorbidity burden, and patient preference.

§7.3.1 — nmCRPC (synthetic)

For patients with non-metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (nmCRPC)
and PSA doubling time of ten months or less, the addition of an
androgen-receptor pathway inhibitor (apalutamide, darolutamide, or enzalutamide)
to ongoing androgen deprivation therapy is recommended. Metastasis-free survival
benefits have been demonstrated in the SPARTAN (apalutamide), ARAMIS
(darolutamide), and PROSPER (enzalutamide) trials.

§8.2.4 — High-risk localized disease (synthetic)

For high-risk localized prostate cancer, both definitive external beam
radiotherapy with long-course androgen deprivation and radical prostatectomy
with extended pelvic lymph node dissection are recommended options. Choice
should be individualized based on patient factors and shared decision making.

§9.1.1 — Radical prostatectomy indications (synthetic)

Radical prostatectomy is a recommended treatment option for patients with
localized prostate cancer of intermediate- or high-risk profile and an estimated
life expectancy exceeding ten years. Extended pelvic lymph node dissection should
be performed when the estimated risk of lymph node involvement exceeds five
percent.

§9.3.2 — Radiotherapy dose and fractionation (synthetic)

For definitive external beam radiotherapy of localized prostate cancer, a total
dose of 74 to 80 Gy delivered with intensity-modulated radiotherapy and
image guidance is recommended. Moderate hypofractionation is an established
alternative in appropriately selected patients.

§10.2.1 — Focal therapy (synthetic)

Focal therapy, including high-intensity focused ultrasound and cryotherapy,
remains investigational for localized prostate cancer and should be offered
within a clinical trial or prospective registry rather than as an established
standard of care.

§11.4.3 — Biochemical recurrence after radical prostatectomy (synthetic)

In patients with biochemical recurrence after radical prostatectomy, salvage
radiotherapy to the prostate bed should be initiated early, at a PSA below 0.5
ng/mL, to maximize the probability of durable biochemical control. The addition
of androgen deprivation therapy may be considered in selected higher-risk cases.

§12.1.1 — Metastasis-directed therapy (synthetic)

In patients with oligometastatic prostate cancer, metastasis-directed therapy
such as stereotactic body radiotherapy may be considered to defer the start of
systemic androgen deprivation therapy, though systemic therapy remains the
standard of care for polymetastatic disease.

§13.2.2 — Bone health during androgen deprivation (synthetic)

Patients receiving long-term androgen deprivation therapy should undergo baseline
assessment of bone mineral density, and bone-protective agents should be offered
to those at elevated fracture risk. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation is
recommended.

§14.1.1 — PARP inhibitors in metastatic castration-resistant disease (synthetic)

In patients with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer harbouring
homologous recombination repair (HRR) gene alterations, particularly BRCA1 or
BRCA2, a PARP inhibitor is recommended following progression on an
androgen-receptor pathway inhibitor.
